Article I: The Mechanics of Urban Monopoly

In the modern urban landscape, the evolution of business models dictates the survival of the mass. Fast food chains often achieve a monopoly by using their capital to penetrate every neighborhood. Their main rival might try to hold one’s own through a variation of style, but the regularity of freight and delivery usually ensures the former’s dominance.

When a monetary crisis occurs, international authorities may locate the source of the lag in the market. If a corporation is too vulnerable, the government might bail them out to prevent a total collapse. Meanwhile, the operation director must spell out every contract to the staffer to avoid a conflict. By and large, the quality of life depends on whether one can get by in this competitive phase. Even if you are on the fence about the ethics of mass consumption, you must face up to the fact that these systems come into effect regardless of individual vote.

Article II: Ecological Integration and the Biological Cost

The World Wildlife Fund continues to highlight the reduction of natural habitat. When we build a corridor or a garage in a previously isolate area, we disturb the constant balance of nature. The integration of human infrastructure—like the street or the garden—often forces animals like the ox or camel to descend into smaller territories.

This is a process of slow destruction, occurring bit by bit. Moreover, the ash from industrial plants can penetrate the basin where we collect water. We must strive to protect the outstanding beauty of the wilderness before it is less than a memory. At at no time should we assume that nature can shake off this attack without help. If we deceive ourselves, the awful truth will eventually clash with our desire for pleasure. We must take heart and back down from total exploitation.

Article III: The Individual within the System

Inside an inn in Fresno or a house in South Carolina, a pupil sits on a stool, looking at a bulb. In the cupboard, there is meat and dairy. Outside, a security guard watches a pedestrian walk across the border of the property. Life is a series of regularity and pause. One might play chess or golf, or perhaps pick up a saw to cut a limb of a tree in half.

If a panic arises—perhaps the sound of a pistol or an urgent call for help—the brain must react. Do you stay and be involved in the solution, or do you see off the danger and sign off for the night? Some might talk through one’s hat about bravery, but on the spot, many cling to what is safe. You might sip your tea and catch one’s breath, but the fundamental anxiety of life remains consistent. Whether you are an elite or a dumb observer, you are bound by the frame of your own journalism—the story you tell yourself to take over your own fear.
